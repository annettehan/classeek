I. Introduction/Motivation

University is a life-changing, rewarding, but challenging experience. The large number and course offerings can cause stress to many students to navigate degree requirements, not only within their major but also the board offerings of electives and other college-specific requirements. Mistakes in understanding course requirements of course sequencing can cost students the additional time needed to complete the degree, hence can cause unexpected and unnecessary monetary cost and delay degree completion. The overwhelming selection of courses can also impede students from exploring other suitable courses they would otherwise enjoy or excel in. Also, the academic consequence (e.g., potential low grades) can impede students from trying and exploring new courses that may align with their academic or personal interests.

In this project, we create a pilot recommender system that reminds students of the courses they should take to meet the college-specific requirements and connect students with courses they would not have otherwise selected. We provide a survey to a student user at the beginning of each section of the system, which asks the student about their course schedule, personal and academic interests, and aspirations. We then use this information to:

  1. Remind the students of the course to take next based on their previous and pending completion, and their academic plan
  2. Recommend elective courses to students that are similar to the student’s input courses and meet LSA degree requirementsRecommend elective courses or Michigan Online courses to students that match
  3. Recommend elective courses or Michigan Online courses to students that match the student’s interests
  4. Recommend courses to students based on the similarity of the student’s schedule and other students
II. Literature review

In higher education settings, course selection is a series of consequential decisions that a student must make to ensure the completion of their academic program requirements. These decisions can be more impactful on students' educational and professional experiences than initially perceived because correct and early decisions can induce efficient study paths and ultimately enhance students' college experience (Babad & Tayeb, 2003). To ensure students' academic success, colleges and universities have made investments in guiding students through understanding the curriculum of their academic program and the course selection process so that students can make the optimal decision in navigating course offerings. Most higher education institutions broadcast their degree offering requirements along with the detailed course catalog on their website to provide accurate information and potential updates. In addition, colleges and universities employ academic advisors and faculty advisors within the individual school and department units to provide recommendations on course and degree selections.

Because of the large number of course offerings, each semester with the everchanging selection in new or revised courses with multiple choices of instructors, modes of instruction, meeting time, and locations, a course recommendation system can make the learning environments more adaptive (Ma et al. 2020), present comprehensive and contemporary course information to students to efficiently select courses and provide schools and departments to publicize offerings and recruits new students.

Course recommendation systems have been introduced and applied in higher education contexts to aid students in navigating the curriculum and their degree's requirements. Most of such systems focus on making predictions such as which courses a student will take (Pardos et al., 2019) or what the grade the student would achieve if enroll (Jiang & Pardos, 2019; Ren, 2019), recommending courses for the upcoming semester by matching courses with students' enrollment history (Pardos et al., 2019) or by following strict rules of students' degree program (Parameswaran et al., 2011). Innovative approaches such as concatenation of course2vec (Pardos et al., 2019) and bag-of-words presentation vector (Pardos & Jiang, 2020), and hybrid method of multiple score framework (Ma et al. 2020) have been proposed. The impact of these algorithms has been studied by surveying voluntary participants (Pardos & Jiang, 2020) or by evaluating model accuracy scores (Lin et al., 2018; Dien et al., 2020).

B. Interest-based Recommender

1. Collaborative filtering

i. Data

Collaborative recommender system was built and evaluated with student_profile.pickle. The user profile was transformed into a user-course sparse matrix with ratings as values. As the collaborative recommender system takes a list of courses that users provide as input, the input list is converted into a sparse vector and appended to the user-course sparse matrix.

ii. Method

Collaborative filtering is an information filtering technique which predicts a user’s interests based on similar users’ interests. The assumptions behind collaborative filtering are that users with similar interests will have similar preferences and vice versa, and a copious amount of user preference data are available for a successful collaborative filtering. Our collaborative recommender systems recommend courses based on similarity between the users. For our collaborative course recommender system, three different collaborative filtering approaches were explored: memory-based approach, and two matrix-based approaches utilizing singular value decomposition (SVD) and non-negative matrix factorization (NMF).

Memory-based approach

Memory-based approach calculates the similarity between users and finds items that a user might be interested in. Our memory-based approach finds similar users based on the cosine similarity. First, we normalize the user-course rating matrix and calculate the cosine similarity between the users. Then, we compute the predicted rating of the target user using the similarity between the target user and the other users as weights. Finally, the desired number of predicted courses are presented to the user.

Matrix-based approach

Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)

The first matrix-based collaborative filtering system employs singular value decomposition. Truncated SVD decomposes the user-course matrix into a product of three matrices, a user-factor matrix, a diagonal matrix of factors, and a factor-course matrix. Then, ratings for unrated courses can be populated by the estimated model generated by the truncated SVD. Based on the populated ratings, recommended courses that are offered in the selected term will be presented to users.

Non-negative Matrix Factorization (NMF)

Non-negative Matrix Factorization (NMF) is another method we tried for our matrix-based collaborative filtering. The idea of employing NMF is similar to the SVD method as it is also utilizing matrix factorization techniques. NMF decomposes the user-course matrix into two non-negative matrices, a user-factor matrix and a course-factor matrix. Then, ratings for unrated courses are generated by the product of the matrices. Finally, the predicted courses are sorted by the ratings, filtered by the selected term, and recommended to users.

iii. Results

The collaborative recommender systems were evaluated with RMSE and runtime. RMSE measures the difference between the model-predicted values and observed values. We calculated the RMSE of each system as an average of 100 random inputs. Table # shows that the memory-based approach achieved the lowest RMSE, but SVD-based approach achieved the shortest runtime. SVD approach can be tuned with n_components, so we further investigated the performance of the SVD with different n_components.

Plot 2 illustrates that the average RMSE decreases and the sum of explained variance increases as n_components increases. However, Plot # shows that the execution time of SVD also linearly increases with n_components. Conservatively speaking, SVD-based filtering method exceeded the runtime of the memory-based approach (~1s) at n_components of 200. However, the average RMSE of SVD with n_components of 200 is 3.0422, which is still higher than the RMSE of the memory-based method, 2.4015. Additionally, the sum of explained variance ratio stayed around 40%. As a result, the memory-based method was selected to be implemented for our collaborative course recommender system.

2.Content-based filtering

a. Survey answer-based recommender

i. Data

Survey results are used as queries for course recommendations. Students are asked to input("Enter your interest 1, the more precise your text is, the more specific the recommendation will be: "), up to 5 interest questions are allowed. Pooled dataset fw.csv is used to create corpus.


ii. Method

Corpus were saved pre-saved, corpus were also pre-trained to obtain the embedding and saved in pkl to save running time when receiving queries. We can generate the embeddings of all documents in the corpus using techniques like BERT. When a new query comes we can convert that to a numerical vector using the same BERT pre-trained model. Candidate documents(descriptions of courses) can then be retrieved based on cosine similarity between query embedding and each of document embeddings.

Computing cosine similarity of query embedding with all document embeddings is very expensive. Libraries like FAISS come into picture. Faiss is a library developed by Facebook AI Research. It is for efficient similarity search and clustering of dense vectors. It allows for very efficient top-k retrieval in embedding space. Essentially document vectors can be indexed offline using a library like FAISS. Now when a new query comes we can convert it to a numerical vector using the same BERT pre-trained model. Query embedding is then used to run nearest neighbour search against the vector index of documents and retrieve top k nearest documents in the embedding space.

iii. Results

For our survey answer-based recommender system, NLP and BERT deep learning methods were used. Thus, semantics of queries and course descriptions were taken into account. Compared to methods based on tf-idf, results are more pertinent and sensitive to the query. However, we could not assess the performance of the system with metrics like precision, recall, or ROC before the deployment and obtaining feedback from real users.

b.Course-based recommender

i.Data

fw.csv was used for the development and the evaluation of the course-based recommender system. We combined text data that contains course information, such as title, subtitle, and description, to calculate the textual similarity between the course information.

ii.Method

Our next recommender system utilizes content-based filtering techniques to recommend courses to users. A content-based recommender system computes the similarity between items and recommends users items similar to what users previously liked. Our content-based recommender system takes a course code that a user wants to find similar courses for and recommends similar courses determined by the similarity between the textual information of the courses. We investigated three different techniques to calculate similarity between texts: count vectorization with cosine similarity, tf-idf vectorization with sigmoid kernel, and a deep learning approach using BERT and Faiss.

Count vectorization + Cosine similarity

The first method utilizes count vectorization and cosine similarity. First, the combined texts are vectorized into count vectors. Then, the cosine similarity between the count vectors of textual information of the input course and the other courses is calculated. Then, the course dataset is sorted based on the similarity measures, filtered with input filters. Lastly, the desired number of course recommendations are presented to the users.

Tf-idf vectorization + Sigmoid kernel

The second method we explored is tf-idf vectorization and sigmoid kernel. In this method, similar steps are taken as the previous method. Instead of count vectorization and cosine similarity measure, however, we vectorized the textual information of the courses into tf-idf vectors and calculated the distance with the sigmoid kernel.

BERT + Faiss

Finally, we applied the deep learning approach that the survey answer-based recommender system was built upon. The only difference between the two systems is that the course-based recommender system takes a course code as an input, and uses the text information of the course to measure similarity between courses instead of user created queries.

iii.Results

The evaluation metrics used for model selection is the topic coherence score and the runtime. Topic coherence score was investigated to examine the consistency in the recommended courses and the input course. The topic coherence scores of descriptions of the recommended courses and the input course as well as the runtimes of the systems are shown in Table 2.

In table 2, the deep learning approach and tf-idf vectorization with sigmoid kernel method achieved higher topic coherence scores than count vectorization with cosine similarity method. However, a clearer difference was observed in runtime performance between the two approaches. The deep learning method achieved a shorter runtime than the tf-idf vectorization and sigmoid kernel method. Consequently, the deep learning method was selected for our course-based recommender system.

V. Discussion

A. Limitations

1. User Profile

The dataset used for collaborative filtering is a simulation of real degree requirements. We did so because we expect real students would have similar structures to their completed course list. We also expect that the restricted used dataset that contains real student information would have similar schemas. For collaborative filtering, the model is built based on the simulated dataset. However, this model can run with the real data of a similar schema. It is also practical to improve/revise the model once restricted used data is used for the recommender.

2. Evaluation of recommendation system

The accuracy and effectiveness of the recommender system are generally accessed by the real-life impact of the recommendations on users' selections rather than the naive accuracy of machine learning models. In an ideal scenario, we would like to obtain users' feedback on the recommendation our recommender systems made to continuously improve and revise the system. The metrics that would need to be evaluated are precision, recall, ROC curves, to name a few. Unfortunately, since the system is new and not yet deployed, we do not have users' feedback data for such assessment. Once the recommenders are deployed and utilized by real users for a substantial time, and a significant volume of feedback received, we plan to use this data to access and improve the recommender systems.

B.Next Steps

We plan to carry out a user study to assess the quality of the recommendations. A user study simulates some use scenarios in reality. We will recruit real users (the system knows the sequence of courses they have taken) after our recommender system is deployed. They will be asked to evaluate the system suggested courses (courses they have not taken yet). After they use the system, We will ask the users multi-faceted questions (e.g., relevance, novelty, diversity, etc.), basically whether they “like” those recommendations. Precision, recall of the system can be then calculated based on the actual feedback from the users.
